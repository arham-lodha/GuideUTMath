# Paths through UT math

<!-- *Draft, to be updated as contributors submit bios. Maintained by the steward.* -->

As undergraduates, we all come to math with different perspectives, and those perspectives shape where we end up. Some people arrive with a clear sense of what they want; others are still figuring out whether math is even the right fit, let alone what comes after graduation. All of these are valid starting points.
The paths below are organized around postgraduate trajectories, since that's often the most useful lens when you're trying to find someone whose experience resembles yours. For each trajectory, we've tried to include contributors with a range of undergraduate experiences, so you can find a thread that resonates.

## Mathematics PhD

You want to pursue doctoral work in mathematics. Contributors in this section came to this decision at different points in their undergrad — some early, some late. Furthermore, the contributors have had a diverse range of experiences regarding when they realized what area of math they would like to choose as their desired area of focus during their graduate studies. We have individuals who realized at least a broad area of study relatively early as a undergraduate student, individuals who realized exactly what they wanted to study "late", and finally individuals are entering graduate school with an extremely broad idea of what they would like to study (which isn't necessarily a bad thing)
The structured responses in [sections/06-grad-school/](/sections/06-grad-school/README.md) are worth reading for further details

## PhD in an Adjacent Field

A PhD in a discipline adjacent to math — physics, computer science, neuroscience, economics, or similar. This path often reflects a second major, interests that broadened outside of math, or a draw toward the intersection of math with another field.

## Master's, not PhD

Pursuing a masters degree is a valid alternative to pursuing a PhD. It can be a stepping stone, a pivot, or even a deliberate alternative to a PhD. Furthermore, pursuing a master's is often a way for international mobility, if one wants to pursue graduate school abroad. This is because in the European system, most Doctoral programs expect a master's coming in. Pursuing a masters is a good way to help you figure out what you want after graduating, if you still don't fully know.
