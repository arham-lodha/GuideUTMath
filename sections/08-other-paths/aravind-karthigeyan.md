# Aravind on taking an adjacent path

I was always physics + math -> theoretical physics.

## The path in one sentence

I started out as a physics and math major, studied the classes that I needed, and I ended up starting a PhD in theoretical physics.

## When I first suspected this was where I was headed, and when I knew

I always wanted a PhD in theoretical physics, at least since high school.

I grew very confident about getting a PhD around my sophomore year. I was flowing with my classes, enjoyed them, was good at them, etc.

## What mattered in undergrad, and what did not

Research, research, research. GPA, what classes you take, extracurriculars, and to a lesser degree tests do sorta matter, but by far it is research. I was told this when I was a freshman. I took it seriously. But I could have always done a bit more. It helps get you close with profs as well.

Those beginning proof-based classes did not matter much to me. Get them over with ASAP since UT/CNS/the Math department is cracking down on prereqs. I wish I had skipped them when I had a chance.

## What the pivot looked like

I didn't pivot.

## What I wish I had known earlier

That I was more mathematically mature and ready for more difficult classes earlier on.

## Who this path is a good fit for

Theoretical physicists who are physics and math doubles. Maybe mathematical physics.

## What a pure-math major should know about the overlap

There is an okay amount of overlap. Less than CS, probably more than any other field, but still a lot of other courses. Still fun courses and I liked my classes, but only a moderate amount of overlap.
