# mathematical maturity, intuition, and the horrors of introductory algebra pedagogy

It took me three entire undergraduate-level algebra classes until I finally understood why groups were a nice thing to study. I didn't even figure it out while I was taking that third algebra class -- it was the summer afterwards, when I learned that a principal $G$-bundle is trivial if and only if it admits a section, that groups finally made sense to me. Did you know that groups were originally defined as subgroups of symmetric groups? The phrase "a group is something that acts like a group" is so useless and tautological, but it rings so much truer than a short list of axioms does once you have the intuition of groups down pat. 

When you take an undergraduate algebra course, they tell you what a group action is and give you some contrived, discrete examples, like the group of symmetries of a platonic solid acting on that platonic solid, and tell you to compute things. It's like you have to go out of your way to find those examples. But groups and group actions arise so naturally, because every example of a group action in the real world is just $\mathrm{Aut}(X)$ acting on $X$, and what the group is really depends on what $X$ is and what the morphisms in the ambient category are, and they never tell you this in an undergraduate algebra class, so it's hard to understand why group actions are actually the most natural thing in the world to study. (At least, it's difficult if you had my lack of mathematical maturity while taking undergraduate algebra...) And then I took graduate algebra and David Ben-Zvi spent half the class abstractmaxxing and it was totally awesome and algebra was good. 

The point I'm trying to make is, it's difficult to have a proper assessment of your own mathematical maturity using only classes as a benchmark. The first algebra class I took, I had a ridiculously high grade (from crazy curves) and came out feeling like I had a wishy-washy understanding of the subject, compared to my real analysis class, which had felt very visual and intuitive. (This is why I went back and took two more undergraduate algebra classes. I feel like you can never know enough algebra, but I might be saying that because I'm bad at algebra.) It's also difficult to have a proper assessment of your own mathematical maturity in general -- if I'm mathematically immature, I won't know what mathematical maturity feels like, and therefore I won't know if I have it! And the other point I'm trying to make is that there are a lot of approaches to learning the same topic, and some might gel better with your intuition than others. Compare and contrast Hatcher vs. May for algebraic topology; their approaches couldn't be more different. So whenever you're struggling to learn something, try putting the book down and finding another one on the same topic. (I can often be found with five tabs on the same topic open in my PDF reader at once.) Or go read about something else. Maybe if you take a break from your algebra studies to read about principal $G$-bundles, you'll come out with a better understanding of algebra!

It is difficult not to notice a gender disparity in the way male and female students are treated and regarded in mathematics which are subtler than the blatant sexism people describe. For example, in my classes, male students are more likely to collaborate and work with each other, and the same is true of female students, but this gives male students an advantage because there are so many more of them. Whenever people talk about enjoying classes where they made friends with their classmates, this always seems to be a floating asterisk in the room that no one seems to want to address. Gendered socialization and the formation of single-sex social circles are predicated on the exclusion of the other sex, and therefore necessarily sexist by definition. When socialization is overly gendered, it inherently excludes people who do not slot neatly into a cisnormative gender binary (i.e. trans and non-binary people), cutting them off from support systems and disadvantaging them. 

I also noticed that male students would be praised for accomplishments (such as taking a particularly rigorous class early on in their degree) and gain self-confidence for it while female students would not receive any praise for comparable achievements and lack confidence. Similarly, I felt that I have received a lot of patronizing disapproval about studying mathematics from outsiders who regard me as wasting my time studying something needlessly difficult and useless (negative perception), while I have had male peers tell me that outsiders tend to regard them as being very intelligent for studying math (positive perception). Competition mathematics (e.g. Putnam, number sense, integral bee) is a particularly egregious setting which exaggerates gender disparities in math. 

At the same time, I have experienced social settings focused around women in math in which the participating members are actually either implicitly or explicitly discouraged from talking about math, since it is seen as discouraging those who know less mathematics. There is a sense in which these types of settings pre-emptively expect women are to struggle, rather than succeed, in their academics. I also feel that some initiatives focused on gender minorities in mathematics are very centered on the experiences of cisgender women. I don't see a lot of discussion about sexism in mathematics that isn't strictly about cis men in math who treat cis women in math poorly, and I would ask everyone to take a more critical lens towards gendered dynamics in not just mathematical, but all social settings. 



# should i try to do a dual major? is this how one performs gay?

Clearly, I have a lot of thoughts about gender as a system that I've been ruminating on since I was 16. Have I ever used these thoughts in an academic context? I actually came into UT wanting to double major in English, but scrapped that idea after I fell into a topology rabbit hole my first semester. I did get to take exactly one English class during my time at UT, and my professor loved me because I am deeply opinionated and a hater, as any good English student must be. 

The title of this section is taken from [this very relatable interview](https://web.archive.org/web/20260419135217/https://blogs.ams.org/inclusionexclusion/2017/10/09/on-performing-queerness-and-mathematics-emily-riehl-interviews-mike-hill/) between Mike Hill and Emily Riehl -- two very big names in my area who are both openly queer. Academics tend to want to express themselves in an intellectual way -- how do you self-express queerness intellectually when all of your energy is being expended on mathematics? What is better "queer praxis", working towards visibility as an openly queer and successful (in this case, Mike Hill levels of successful!) mathematician or being more well-read about queer theory? 

It is a very human instinct to want to create art, which is also a form of intellectual self-expression. I kept worrying that academia wouldn't work out as a career path for me, and considered careers in the creative industry as an alternative option. This is a pretty stupid backup, because people work their asses off to get into the creative industry, and the working conditions are terrible. I am not cut out for late-stage capitalism and crunch culture. But I love media and narratives and stories, so whenever I had free space in my schedule, I took a class centered on media. I even did a media internship for credit hours at one point. Probably, this did all distract from the mathematics I maybe should have spent that time doing, but I'm not really feeling a lot of regret about it. I want to be making queer art, even if I am not able to make a living from it. It's hard to balance math with your other interests, and it's easy to feel like math is taking over your whole life, so it's worthwhile to put in the effort to find a compromise that works for you.
