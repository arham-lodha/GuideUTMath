# Real talk

**Format:** individual essays, kept in each contributor's voice with minimal editing.

The stuff that doesn't fit anywhere else and that most advice documents leave out.

## What goes here

- Falling in and out of love with math — what it looks like, how to tell, what to do next
- Imposter syndrome and comparison, including the specific version of it that hits when your cohort is very strong
- Burnout and pacing
- Mental health during grad school application season
- What "success" looked like from the outside vs. the inside
- What you'd do differently, at a life-scale rather than a tactics-scale

## Format

Individual essays, 500–2000 words, in your voice. Write what you'd want to tell yourself as a sophomore. No template. No word-count games.

## What this section is for

Two specific future readers:

1. A current UT math major who is struggling and can't tell whether they're struggling because the material is hard (normal) or because this path isn't for them (also normal and fine).
2. A current UT math major who is succeeding, and feels terrible anyway, and doesn't understand why nobody else seems to feel this way.

Both of these readers exist every year. The content that helps them almost never gets written because the people who could write it are (a) busy, (b) worried about how it looks, or (c) already past it and embarrassed by who they were when they needed it.

Please write anyway.

## A note on privacy

If you want to write under your initials, a pseudonym, or anonymously, that's fine — just tell the steward. The steward keeps a private record of authorship in case anyone ever needs to revise or remove a piece, but the public document honors whatever attribution level you ask for.
