# Ren Watson on the NSF GRFP

*Written May 2026, graduated Spring 2026, now at UW-Madison for Math PhD.*  
*Applied 2026, outcome awarded. I also received an internal fellowship from my PhD program, and I have included some information about those at the bottom.*

## What was your research proposal about, in plain language?

I wrote about calculating the moments (particularly focusing on expected value and variance) of the cardinalities of the product set and left and right quotient set of a uniformly randomly chosen finite subset of an infinite nonabelian group, focusing on some specific infinite nonabelian groups with particularly nice structure for this problem. The probabilities needed to compute these moments are not independent of one another, and my proposal focused on extending an existing technique used to address the analogous question for the group of integers which accounts for the dependence between these probabilities by considering an equivalent problem in graph theory. My proposal explored how understanding the graph theory problems associated to these infinite nonabelian groups poses some new challenges that did not exist in the previous abelian case of the integers and argued for how resolving these problems could still be made feasible by exploiting the structures of the particular groups I was considering.

## How did you land on that topic, given when you applied?

This project was tangentially related to one of the projects I had worked on at my most recent REU, and I had started considering questions related to this proposal with one of my collaborators at the very end of the REU for some specific nonabelian groups. Because of this, I had some preliminary results to include in my proposal that illustrated how to think about the graph theory problems in the nonabelian case, and I think being able to write about some concrete results (even if they were only for some of the simplest cases of what I was proposing) was really helpful to my proposal. I think that, seeing as I was applying as a senior undergrad and not as a grad student, writing a proposal for a project related to a successful REU project that had produced a paper (and was related to the research interests that I was applying to grad school to pursue) seemed like a strong move.

## What was the argument of your personal statement?

I did 3 REUS and one other research experience during my undergraduate career, so the majority of the introduction and intellectual merit portions of my personal statement were dedicated to giving an overview of those experiences and creating a cohesive narrative out of how my research interests emerged out of them. I emphasized publications, major conference presentations, and my contributions to all of the REU projects I worked on.  

## What did you do about broader impacts?

In my personal statement, I focused on my more general teaching and mentoring aspirations I had as someone who ultimately wants to become a professor. I strongly emphasized how having a ton of opportunities to present at conferences through my various research experiences during undergrad had helped me develop the skill of explaining math in a way that is tailored to the familiarity level of my audience to the topic and how this skill will help me in the future as a mentor and teacher.

For the broader impacts section of my research proposal, I talked a little about how I thought many research problems in combinatorics and combinatorial number theory can serve as approachable introductions for undergrads to pure math research because the motivation behind them is often pretty simple to state and understand (compared to areas of math that require substantially more upfront work to even understand the objects being studied), even if they are quite difficult to solve. I discussed how, in particular, the project I proposed was nice for mentorship purposes because it helps motivate how some of the concepts you learn in an undergraduate probability or algebra course come up and can interact in research. I also proposed that learning some of the probability and graph theoretic techniques that would be helpful in resolving parts of this project could make for a nice Directed Reading Project that I could run as a graduate mentor. Finally, I talked about why the type of problem I proposed is worth studying and highly applicable.

## Who read your drafts, and how many drafts did you go through?

I didn't keep track of how many drafts I went through. I originally wrote long versions of both my personal statement and research proposal that went  over the length requirements, and then I edited them both down mostly by removing unnecessary mathematical details and sticking to more overview-level mathematical explanations. I had my sister who was studying writing in college review my drafts for wording clarity and to neaten up my prose. I checked in with some of my recommenders about the project idea but didn't ask them to edit my drafts because I felt bad at the time that I had just requested so many rec letters from them and didn't want to add another thing to their plates, so all of the mathematical and content editing I did myself.

## What would you do differently?

It's definitely a good idea to have more people edit your drafts than I did. Since the rec letter instructions want your recommenders to write specifically about the project you proposed, it's also a good idea to have drafts sent to them in advance so that they have plenty of time to write your rec letter, so in a perfect world, I would have loved to get my first drafts done earlier so I could have given my rec letter writers more time.

## Advice to a junior thinking about applying in the fall.

This is a research fellowship, so prioritize recommenders who can speak to your research capabilities. Ask your recommenders well in advance and, as I mentioned in the above question, have drafts of your application materials to give them to help them write a strong letter for you. If you have past research that you can relate your project proposal to, that's generally a good idea. For the broader impacts section, the language around the criteria used to assess broader impact has changed in the past couple years, so make sure to check the current criteria. There are examples of past successful proposals that past winners have made available online that are great resources to get some inspiration for how to structure your proposal, but some of them have broader impacts sections on these may not be reflective of the current criteria under which broader impacts are assessed.

## Internal PhD program fellowships

In addition to the GRFP, I was offered several fellowships that are internal to the institutions that I was accepted to for PhD. Consideration for these is typically automatic when you apply to the PhD program, and institutions generally award them to top choice applicants as an additional incentive to attend their program. Some internal fellowships are purely financial (i.e., you are still expected to TA, but your stipend will be higher than the base stipend for a graduate TAship), while others also reduce or eliminate TA expectations for some or all of your time as a PhD student. Some of the internal fellowships I was offered were decided based on my PhD application materials, and for others, the math department reached out to me to say I had received an initial nomination and ask for an additional personal statement before making a final decision. In my experience, often times it is the math department that nominates you but the larger school of graduate studies or something similar that makes the final calls on who gets it. It varies by institution, but you are typically able to accept both an internal fellowship and the GRFP (during my PhD, I will be on GRFP funding for 3 years and internal fellowship from UW-Madison for 2 years).
