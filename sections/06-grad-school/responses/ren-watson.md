# Ren Watson on grad school applications

*Written May 2026, graduated Spring 2026, now at UW-Madison mathematics PhD.*

## Where did you apply, and roughly how did you build the list?

I applied to 20 PhD programs + Cambridge part III master's. I started from a list of every single math PhD program in the country because I was anxious about missing a lower name recognition program that could be a good fit (this approach is very effective in some sense but also very time-consuming). I immediately eliminated all the ones in locations I did not wish to spend 5 years in and then did a skim of professor research interests from everywhere that was left and eliminated all the ones that didn't have at least 2 good matches. This left me with a medium list of 40-50, and then I ran that by my recommenders and advisors and they helped me narrow it down to the final list.

## Who wrote your letters, how did you pick them, and when did you ask?

I used my two most recent REU mentors, both of whom know my research really well and run extremely well-known REUs that have a history of getting students into top grad schools. My third letter was from a UT professor with whom I had taken multiple classes (both undergrad and grad) in an area related to my interests. I had pre-asked both of my REU advisors about writing me a grad school letter during those REUs, and I asked my UT professor and followed up with both of my REU advisors in October. 

## What was the core argument of your statement of purpose?

My research experience and number of publications was the most standout part of my application, so my statements of purpose focused a lot on each of my REUs and how my research interests evolved from them, as well as the coursework (at UT and abroad at BSM) and independent studies/DRPs that were most relevant to those interests. I tried to make it clear how successful all of the REU projects I participated in were, how my contributions were crucial to this success, and generally how I was very well-prepared for the research demands of grad school. I had a core part of my statement of purpose that I used on each of my applications (one version that was more tailored to programs that were more number theory-focused, one for those that were more combinatorics-focused, since I was applying for both), and then I filled in 1-2 paragraphs at the end with details about what (mostly particular professors' research) made me a particularly good fit for whichever particular program.

## GRE and Math GRE: what did you do, and would you do it again?

I started to study for the math GRE my junior year and found it to be the single most boring mathematical thing I had done in a very long time. So, I went and checked my (at the time) medium list of programs I potentially wanted to apply to, realized that by chance very few of them required (and very few recommended) the math GRE, and made the choice to not continue to bore myself. I don't necessarily recommend this approach, since it is a bit risky (I was gambling a bit because I was using what programs had done the previous application cycle and assuming they would likely keep the same policy for my application cycle), but I will say that I got offers from multiple programs that recommended the math GRE despite not submitting a score. I did not take the regular GRE, and none of the programs I applied to cared about it.

## Interviews and visits: what were they actually like?

I did 3 interviews, all of which were relatively relaxed. One was with two different professors who were in different Zoom rooms, one was with a panel of current professors and current grad students, and one was with just the head of the graduate program. They all seemed to be more about checking personality and fit and were more of a final screening thing for short-list applicants than a super judgmental thing. I visited 4 programs (UW-Madison, Georgia Tech, USC, and Vanderbilt), two over my spring break and 2 over weekends during the semester. They were all honestly super informative. It was really lovely to meet professors and current students, and I feel like I learned way more from my visits than I could have from any amount of internet research. I ended up eliminating my second-choice program from a visit because I realized while I was there that it wasn't a great fit for me specifically (not many non-men in the department, amongst some other factors), and I ultimately made the choice to attend UW-Madison from my visit (I was leaning towards going there when I visited, but the visit really confirmed good vibes, and I committed to the program before I even got back to Texas from my visit).

## How did you make your final decision?

Cambridge kind of automatically got eliminated from my consideration because they didn't offer me money and international student tuition is expensive lol. I narrowed down the set of PhD programs I got into to a top 4 that I visited. This was partially based on prestige (because I would really love a job in academia after my PhD, and while I don't like how much prestige weighs into the odds of that happening, I acknowledge that it does) but also based on who offered me/nominated me for internal fellowships and where I wanted to live for 5 years. I talked to each of my recommenders and some other professors about what I should do, and I got in touch with friends/friends of friends at each school to talk to them about the experience of being in the math department at each. I **loved** 3/4 of the programs I visited but chose UW-Madison because my recommenders suggested it would be the best choice for me in terms of both fit and career outcome, because I loved the math department from visiting, and because I was offered internal fellowship that offset the fact that the base stipend offer from UW-Madison was lower than some of the other programs I was considering. The major other lingering concern I had before picking UW-Madison is that they've (at the time of my writing this) increased TA workload a bit in response to funding shortages and taking a substantially smaller cohort in my cycle than usual, but since I ended up having fellowship support for all 5 years between internal and GRFP and if you choose the TA on fellowship at UW the workload expectation is lower than if you're funded by TAship, this ended up being a non-concern.

## What did you get wrong or waste time on?

I was really stressed about how the application cycle would go the year I applied because of NSF funding shortages, so I feel like I played it very safe in where I decided to apply and applied to too many safeties. I think I also wasted a lot of time agonizing over really small details of wording choices in my personal statements that definitely didn't make any difference in where I got in. 


## One thing you'd tell yourself in September of senior year.

Calm down.
