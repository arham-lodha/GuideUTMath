  list-building: |
    Where did you apply, and roughly how did you build the list? (~250 words)

    I applied to 12 PhD programs and one master's program (which was my only safety). I didn't want to go to schools in red states, and mostly applied to schools in places that I thought I might like. I applied for algebraic topology and homotopy theory, but I didn't really want to do higher category theory, homotopy type theory, or algebraic K-theory, so I cut a lot of schools where the algebraic topology research was in these areas, and this easily whittled it down to 12. I didn't have any professors to tell me where to apply (I would ask them and they would say, "I don't do alg top, I can't help you" lol), so I found schools by going through the top list and checking to see if they had algebraic topology + by looking up famous algebraic topologists, their institutions, and their coauthors' institutions. It turns out there aren't that many schools with advisors who I want to work with; I'd apply if there was one person whose work I was interested in. I would also be more likely to apply if I could attain an application fee waiver. I was accepted into three PhD programs and the master's program (which offered me PhD admission anyway), so I think the list I built did a great job of giving me several excellent options to choose from without spending too much of my money. At the start of the spring, I was wishing I had applied to more schools because of the radio silence I was receiving, but there are safety schools that keep PhD applications pretty late into the spring, and that helped quell some of my anxieties. 

  letters: |
    Who wrote your letters, how did you pick them, and when did you ask? (~250 words)

    I asked my advisor who I'm writing my thesis with and previously read with, the professor of my algebraic topology prelim, and my REU advisor from over the summer. I asked mostly in August and September. I wasn't originally planning on asking my prelim professor, but I was told I should get a letter from someone I took a properly graded class with in my area. I feel that UT somewhat disadvantaged me in terms of letters, in this sense; the algebraic topology 2 course which is offered occasionally does not fall under this category, so this meant I had to ask a professor from my sophomore year, and I was worried that my letter writer's perception of my mathematical abilities might be outdated. Moreover, I was applying for algebraic topology, but there weren't any algebraic topologists at UT for me to a) learn algebraic topology from and b) vouch for my algebraic topology abilities as an algebraic topologist. 

  sop: |
    What was the core argument of your statement of purpose? (~300 words)

    I feel like my SOP was slop and it would be dishonest to act like it had a "core argument". I pretty much described all the algebraic topology I had done for several paragraphs until I looked like I knew what I was talking about (+ dedicated one chunky paragraph to geometric topology and one short paragraph to algebra). I was told to look like I know what I was talking about, so I got really specific about some of the things that I'd learned. I wrote one draft, sent it to one of my profs who told me to make the opening paragraph stronger/more of a summary in order to get information across faster to bored admissions committees, but had no comments otherwise. I did as he said and pretty much didn't change it again after that. 

  gre: |
    GRE and Math GRE: what did you do, and would you do it again? (~150 words)

    I took the mGRE in October of 2025 because UCLA and Columbia required it. I don't remember what I got, but it wasn't that great, maybe in the low 70s percentile. That being said, I was very excited when I saw my score was in the low 70s percentile, because I genuinely thought I had failed the test -- I was really busy in my senior fall and hadn't had much time to study, and the specific test I took ended up being honestly ridiculously difficult (moreso than usual, from what I've heard) with lots of random niche topics... which I preferred to the really hard calculus questions that were ALSO on the test. I got stumped on lots of integrals, my brain was melting, and I had to add/multiply so many big numbers without a calculator, it was terrible. (Apparently UCLA did not gaf because I got in anyway -- if anything, it's good to take the mGRE just to check that box?)

  interviews: |
    Interviews and visits: what were they actually like? (~200 words)

    I didn't have any interviews. I think interviews aren't that common in math because mathematical ability and social skills are inversely correlated. 

    ...that's my go-to joke that I say to non-math people, but after talking to some of them, I see why math programs don't typically interview; science research is structured in a way that requires a lot more collaboration with PIs and labmates, so interviews are actually necessary for those kinds of programs. 

    I went to two visits. Mostly, we had short one-on-one or two-on-one meetings with professors in or adjacent to (or not so adjacent to) my area, and some time to chill around with the other students. There were info sessions (slideshows about administrative stuff) as well as math talks (from grad students or faculty). One of the math talks was given by a homotopy theory professor and it was sooo fire. Genuinely cinema

  decision: |
    How did you make your final decision? (~250 words)

    I received offers from three PhD programs and one master's program (safety) which offered me PhD admission anyway. I turned down the master's program immediately due to less aligned research interests, bad location, and worse reputation than my PhD offers (which I received first). I was concerned with the reputations of the institutions because academia as an institution is quite elitist, and it was harder to see myself getting a job in academia if I turned down a big name offer. The postdoc placement rates of one of the PhD programs I was considering scared me off, and that program has SO many researchers in my area (read: four and a half), including one of the biggest names. The other program was from a non-U.S. school with a very good reputation, but the research was more niche (despite being very interesting), and the research interests of everyone in the country that school was in were not that aligned with mine. I was afraid of being stuck in that research niche, so I chose UCLA, where the research is more computational and mainstream, so I think it would be easier to switch to other things. All of the offers I received had vaguely terrible stipends, so that was not really a factor in my decision. Also, all of the programs I received offers from had a really great selection of graduate course offerings, especially compared to UT :(

  mistakes: |
    What did you get wrong or waste time on? (~200 words)

    I definitely could have studied for the mGRE more. I probably should have done more research on certain schools and cut them from my list earlier (out of algebraic K-theory phobia). I was also REALLY busy that semester and I probably would have committed to less if I did it over again. I wouldn't call this a mistake, but as I have said a few times, I felt that trying to study algebraic topology at UT might have disadvantaged me in my grad admissions process, and I think I would have been a more conventionally accomplished undergraduate if I spent that time studying something that people here actually do (read: geometric topology) and then switched over to alg top in grad school. I also changed my letter writer list last minute to someone I had had less interaction with than my original planned letter writer, as mentioned above, and I am not sure if this weakened my application. That being said, saying things like "I would have been more successful if I did xyz" is a loser mentality, and stable homotopy theory is an incredibly beautiful subject that I will never regret spending time on studying :)

  advice-to-self: |
    One thing you'd tell yourself in September of senior year. (~100 words)

    I would have really warned myself about how rough the spring of the grad admissions cycle is! Waiting for decisions and then choosing a school both take a pretty big mental toll on you. I was really thinking "the hard part is over" when I submitted all my applications, but I was totally wrong.
