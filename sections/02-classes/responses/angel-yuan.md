# Angel on classes

*Written April 2026, graduated 2026, now at UCLA.*

## What was your rough course sequence, and how did you land on it? (~250 words)

1st year: Undergrad topology sequence, linear algebra

2nd year: Undergraduate algebra sequence, graduate topology sequence

3rd year: Graduate algebra 1, graduate complex analysis, lots of grad topics classes

4th year: Graduate real analysis

## Which class taught you the most, and why? (~200 words)

The algebraic topology prelim course (M 382C) was great because of the content. It feels very gratifying and beautiful to study something where you can lead with so much visual intuition and picture-drawing. I feel like I didn't understand group actions before I learned covering space theory; algebraic topology is where algebra finally clicks. 

I also learned a lot from Closed Smooth 4-Manifolds (M 392C) with Lisa Piccirillo. I took it my junior spring, and I had already decided that I wouldn't go into low-dimensional topology, so I hadn't studied that much of the subject before I took the class, and when I did take the class, it was like being thrown into the deep end in order to learn how to swim. (She made me do homework and no one else; at some point I turned in screenshots of Gompf-Stipsicz that I had annotated on MS paint.) In spite of this, Lisa was very patient with me and open to my stupid questions. Anyway, I learned a lot and it was awesome, but I was also lowkey on the verge of tears half the time. 

## Which class do you wish you'd taken, or taken earlier? (~150 words)

If I could, I would have taken grad complex analysis my freshman year -- it was taught by a good professor that semester, I had a lot of friends in that class, and I had a lot of free time back then. I probably wouldn't have been let into the class, though. I ended up taking it my junior year and the professor covered pretty much nothing. 

## Which class do you wish you hadn't taken, or taken later? (~150 words)

I took the differential topology prelim in Spring 2024 and it was taught by a geometer who wore the squeakiest shoes known to man, so I had an all-around awful experience. If I had taken it the year after, it would have been with Irving, who is the best lecturer in the department. That being said, I was able to take certain topics courses in my junior year that I probably wouldn't have been able to take if I had put off the diff top prelim. 

## Grad courses as an undergrad: when and why? (~200 words)

I took 11 grad courses, starting from my sophomore fall. I had mostly run out of undergraduate courses to take, although I ended up re-taking undergraduate algebra that same fall (I had taken undergraduate algebra in high school). I had done a DRP in algebraic topology the semester before I took the prelim course, so I had already learned a solid chunk of the material coming in, and was really wanting to learn more. I was told that it's weird (/neg) on grad admissions to not take grad courses as an undergrad if you want to go to grad school for math. 

## Reading courses: did you do them, how did they work? (~200 words)

I did a reading course with Irving on vector bundles, but it wasn't really specifically on vector bundles so much as it was about "whatever topology" (which ended up being vector bundles). It was initially going to be on low-dimensional topology, except I quickly decided that it wasn't my thing and switched back to algebraic topology. Vector bundles are incredibly important in both algebraic and geometric topology, so it was a really good thing for me to have learned then; I use them all the time. 

## One piece of tactical advice about coursework. (~100 words)

I feel like it doesn't really help that much to listen to people's advice on whether courses are easy or hard. People told me that undergrad algebra with Keel would be hard, and that algebraic topology would be hard, and that grad algebra would be hard, and none of those courses were very hard when I took them. Conversely, people told me differential topology would be easy, and that ended up being hell on earth :(
