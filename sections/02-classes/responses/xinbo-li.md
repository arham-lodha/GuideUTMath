# Xinbo Li on classes

*Written April 2026, graduated 2026, now at Duke University.*

## What was your rough course sequence, and how did you land on it? (~250 words)

In my freshman year, I took the basic courses: vector calculus (honors), linear algebra, discrete (honors), and differential equations (honors). For honors courses other than vector calculus, you have to fill an application. This is just for general education and learning prerequisites for more advanced courses. 

Next year I took algebra 1&2, real analysis 1, and complex analysis (a topics course). Originally the plan is to build a broad foundation in math, but I quickly fell in love with the language of algebra. That structural perspective began guiding my course selection.

As junior, I took graduate algebra 1, real analysis 2, topology 1, differential topology, and a reading course on Lie group and Lie algebra. My goal was still to "round out" my general education with measure theory, topology, and manifolds. I actually registered for Differential Topology mainly because a good friend dragged me into it! By this point, I knew I wanted to study geometry, but *my* definition of geometry was still very vague. 

In my senior year, I took algebraic topology, representation theory (with both Ben--Zvi and Mason--Brown), Riemann surfaces, "open problems in topology", and a tutorial course (leading to a thesis). Aside from algebraic topology, which is for general education, I chose these classes specifically to search for my geometric niche. 

## Which class taught you the most, and why? (~200 words)

Vector calculus with Professor Beckner and Algebra 1 with Professor Keel. The vector calculus course contains so much information that even when I am taking much more advanced courses, the ideas and perspectives still appear very frequently. Algebra 1 changed my perception of mathematics. Without the course, I might have already quitted math!

## Which class do you wish you'd taken, or taken earlier? (~150 words)

Topology and algebraic topology -- mainly because I wish to take more graduate topics courses!

## Grad courses as an undergrad: when and why? (~200 words)

I started taking graduate courses when I am in third year. First, you must know how to write proofs, and some sort of "mathematical maturity". It is hard to define "mathematical maturity". I think a good test is to try to pick up a well-regarded textbook and just read: if you can roughly organize the arguments in your head and know what they are doing, you will be fine! Otherwise you are probably not ready. Other than this soft requirement, there are requirements that are reinforced by the department. For example, before you take a graduate topics course (for credit) you must have taken two or more graduate prelim courses and had good grades already. I think it is more beneficial to have a solid understanding of foundational materials before one goes on to take graduate courses.   

## Reading courses: did you do them, how did they work? (~200 words)

I took a reading course with Professor Beckner on Lie groups and Lie algebras, and one with Professor Perutz on Hodge theory. You have to talk to the professor you want to do a reading course with, and then email to Dr. Austin to sign up. 

## One piece of tactical advice about coursework. (~100 words)

Once you know what you like, start choose the courses that teach that exact subject. Also if possible, choose courses on adjacent fields as well to "enrich your toolkit". For example, if you are interested in algebraic geometry, you may try representation theory --- there could be much more algebraic geometry than you think of!
