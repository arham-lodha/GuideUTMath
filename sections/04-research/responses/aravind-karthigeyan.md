  research-order: |
    What research did you do as an undergrad, in what order? (~250 words)

    Lai Group Experimental Condensed Matter: For my first two years, I got experience working in an experimental physics group. I made new compounds, observed them with experimental tools, and got experience and rec letters.

    QuAHNtum Theoretical Condensed Matter: For my junior and senior years, I started to do research in topological condensed matter theory. My main project was studying how Stiefel-Whitney classes can be used to describe materials that had certain symmetries that normal Chern-Simons theory said were topologically trivial. I used a lot of asymptotic analysis, homotopy theory, and characteristic classes of real vector bundles in order to describe this new phase of matter and how it can be experimentally probed for its topological invariants. I did the theory to see how it can be experimentally testable.

    MIT REU for summer 2025. The experimental work an EECS group had done was complete, so I was there to accurately model and improve experimental results. I used methods from many-body field theory for the physics. The system was basically a strongly coupled magnet-cavity system that was pumped with microwaves, and I ended up using a lot of theory from dynamical systems to find nonlinear and chaotic behavior of the system that can be used as an efficient source of probabilistic bits. I also used some cryptographic protocols to use these bits in a protocol, and I made proposals on how this can be used as an efficient source for entangled qubits if cooled.

  day-to-day: |
    What did "doing research" actually look like day-to-day? (~200 words)

    First bit was reading some fundamental material, so maybe a week on that. Next week, I'd focus on the most relevant papers adjacent to the field. Then the actual fun of research happens. I would know the end goal of what I wanted to do and slowly try things until they stuck, with me changing things if I needed to and things not working out. This would be divided into several steps as well. I would usually have an idea with say 3 main steps to get to the end. After I was happy with my results, I would divide each individual step into more steps, making my results more rigorous and less likely to be scrutinized. Sometimes I would see an error in my own logic, but usually that's not disastrous and it is relatively easy to fix. I do this until each step is well trivial. I also use Mathematica and Julia to do some symbolic computation, diagonalize matrices, and to verify my analytic results numerically.

  what-you-got: |
    What did you get out of your research experiences - honestly? (~200 words)

    I found the fields of math and physics that I really like. I also got to meet quite a few people that are like me and share the same curiosity. I also understand more what it's like to be a grad student. It's also pretty fulfilling and it just scratches an itch I did not know I had.

  when-you-did-not-like-it: |
    What did you do when you didn't like what you were doing? (~250 words)

    Most research problems usually have multiple threads you need to solve. If I didn't like what I was doing, I'd switch to a different thread, hope that works out, and get less frustrated afterward. This hasn't really happened in an extreme way to me though.

  tactical-advice: |
    One piece of tactical advice for someone trying to get into research. (~150 words)

    Keep on trying and putting yourself out there. Also pick on the new faculty. They're younger, know more about what you're going through, and at least sometimes could use another brain.
