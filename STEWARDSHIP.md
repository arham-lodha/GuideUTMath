# Stewardship

*This document is for current and future stewards of the Hitchhiker's Guide to UT Math. It is the institutional memory that doesn't fit anywhere else: decisions made and why, things tried that didn't work, conventions worth preserving, and open questions for the next steward to resolve.*

*If you're reading this because you just inherited the guide: welcome. Read this whole file before changing anything structural. Most of what's here exists because someone tried the alternative and it went badly.*

---

## What the steward does

The steward is the person who keeps the guide alive between cohorts. Concretely:

- Reviews and merges contributions (light editing for formatting, typos, broken links — not voice)
- Drafts synthesized sections when there are enough contributor responses to do so
- Maintains the repo and the contributor list
- Runs the annual ritual: reminding the graduating cohort to contribute, soliciting from the current cohort, updating the "paths not represented" list
- Hands off to a successor before graduating

The steward is **not**:
- The sole author of the document (the contributors are)
- An editor of contributor voice (preserve it; correct only typos and factual errors)
- Responsible for making every section comprehensive (gaps are fine; flag them for future cohorts)

## Expected time commitment

Based on the 2026 cohort's experience:

- Initial setup (structure, templates): one intense week, then a long tail
- Each annual cycle: a few hours across the spring semester for outreach and collection, a weekend to synthesize and publish
- Handoff: a semester of shadowing by the incoming steward

If it's taking substantially more than this, something has drifted — probably toward over-editing or over-synthesizing. Back off.

## Succession

The handoff is the single biggest long-term risk to this document. Here's how it should work:

1. Each fall, the current steward identifies 1–2 juniors who seem plausible as future stewards. Signals: they're writing in the Discord, they've contributed or want to, they think about the cohort rather than just themselves.
2. In spring semester, the steward invites the candidate to shadow — meaning they get repo access, sit in on the editorial passes, and start doing small maintenance.
3. Before the steward graduates, the incoming steward formally takes over and updates the name in the README.
4. Outgoing steward stays reachable for questions for at least six months.

If a year goes by without a successor, the document is at risk of becoming stale. Acceptable recovery paths: find a recent graduate willing to steward remotely for one more cycle, or recruit a current faculty member or grad student who cares.

## Decisions made in the first cohort (2026) and why

**Hybrid format (structured responses + synthesized + individual essays) rather than pure either/or.** Reasoning: synthesis alone flattens voice and becomes hard for future stewards to maintain; pure individual voice becomes repetitive and uneven in coverage. The hybrid lets each topic use the format that suits it. If a future steward wants to rework this, do it deliberately — don't let the mix drift by accident.

**GitHub rather than Notion / Google Docs / a custom site.** Reasoning: version control, natural PR review process, math-major-friendly, free, durable. Notion drifts toward private, Google Docs rot, custom sites break. If contributors struggle with GitHub, the steward can accept submissions by email and commit on their behalf.

**No AI-generated content in the document itself.** Claude (or similar tools) can be used for infrastructure — templates, READMEs, site scaffolding, planning — but every piece of content that appears under a human contributor's name must be written by that human. Synthesized sections are drafted by the steward, in their own voice, from real responses. This matters because the document's entire value proposition is "real people wrote this"; LLM prose has a texture that readers recognize, and losing that texture would quietly destroy the thing that makes the guide different from a generic advice blog.

**Attribution and dating of everything.** No anonymous advice (unless a contributor specifically requests it for sensitive content, and the steward keeps a private authorship record). Every piece of content is dated because advice ages — the 2026 state of the math GRE is not the 2029 state.

**"Paths not represented" is stated explicitly rather than faked.** The 2026 cohort had no industry / quant / teaching / actuarial contributors. Rather than writing thin sections to cover them, we left those as explicit gaps. Future stewards: resist the temptation to have Claude or yourself fill in these gaps with generic advice. An honest gap is better than inauthentic coverage.

**The "real talk" section exists.** Mental health, burnout, and falling out of love with math were originally going to be folded into "other paths," but were broken out because they apply across paths and because the framing of "other paths" implied they belonged to people who left, which is wrong. If you're considering reorganizing, think carefully before merging this back in.

**Dean's Scholars section is separate from the math department honors thesis.** The two programs are distinct, the bio/chem tension is specific to Dean's Scholars, and conflating them obscures useful information. Keep them distinct.

## Things tried that didn't work

*This section will grow over time. Future stewards: add to it.*

- [2026] Considered asking contributors to share successful grad school and fellowship essays. Trashed because most contributors weren't comfortable, and the ask was creating awkwardness disproportionate to the content's value. Could be revisited in a future cohort if a contributor volunteers; don't make it a general ask.

## Open questions for future stewards

- **Essays.** Whether to revisit the "share successful essays" idea in a future cohort, and if so, how to structure the ask.
- **Industry paths.** How to solicit contributions from graduates in industry, finance, teaching, actuarial work. The cohort-based model doesn't capture these well because they often have less coordinated graduation experiences.
- **Non-senior contributions.** Whether juniors and sophomores should contribute real-time perspectives (e.g., "what it's like to be currently deciding on an REU") or whether the guide should stay senior-authored. Both have tradeoffs.
- **Cross-cohort synthesis.** At some point, enough cohorts will have contributed that the synthesized sections will need re-drafting. How to handle that without losing earlier contributors' voices.
- **Private / gated content.** Whether there should be a cohort-only layer for content that's too sensitive for a public repo (e.g., specific professor horror stories, frank discussion of lab dynamics). Current answer: no, because gated content rots. But revisit if it keeps coming up.

## Conventions worth preserving

- First-person contributor voice, minimally edited.
- No anonymous advice without explicit opt-in and a private authorship record.
- Dated everything.
- "Don't Panic" stays on the README.
- Stewards are named in the README with graduation year.
